AI assistance changed the development process in two clear ways: speed and decision quality. If we had written the whole robot controller manually, we would have spent much longer just building the first working version of the motion logic, servo control, and HTTP command mapping. With AI, we were able to generate a structured starting point much faster and move directly into testing on the physical kit. That mattered because the hardest problems were not purely coding problems. They appeared only after we uploaded the program and observed how the real robot behaved.

The most useful part of AI support was not “writing everything for us”, but giving us a fast draft that we could adapt. The AI response suggested a modular design with separate hardware, decision, and control layers. That made it easier to debug the project step by step. For example, we could test the ultrasonic scan, then the claw sequence, then the ram-kick behaviour, instead of changing the whole file blindly. AI also helped us think about lightweight edge intelligence, so we introduced adaptive profile selection rather than a fixed one-size-fits-all action.

At the same time, manual improvement was still essential. The generated idea had to be tuned for our actual robot: the claw servo angle, the head center offset, the Wi-Fi fallback behaviour, and the reward rules all needed human adjustment. In other words, AI reduced the time needed to reach a strong prototype, but the final quality still depended on our debugging, calibration, and understanding of the hardware. Compared with manual coding alone, AI made the workflow more iterative, faster to start, and more focused on testing real behaviour.
